HEMS Availability & Unit Disposition Aid

Predicts how likely an air medical aircraft is to be available — factoring thunderstorms, icing, ceiling, visibility, and flight category — and translates that into what to do with your ground unit: commit it to transport, hold it on standby, or clear it to release for other tasks.

What this is for: when weather makes a flight unlikely, you have to provide ground transport — so there's no reason to tie up the unit waiting. When weather is favorable, you may be able to free that unit for other calls. This aid reads weather (thunderstorms, icing, ceiling, visibility, flight category) into a fast availability-confidence read to support that decision. It does not account for aircraft status — whether the bird is already flying, down for maintenance, or the crew is timed out — so always reconfirm real-time availability with dispatch/OCC. It does not make the flight decision either; the pilot-in-command and operations control center own that. Treat a "release" call as your read, not a guarantee.

Icing — Quick Reference

Icing is not in a routine METAR. Most HEMS helicopters in this market are not certified for flight into known icing, so forecast or reported icing in the layer the aircraft must climb/descend through is a knockout — even under a clear surface picture. The practical trigger is the freezing level: if it's at or below the altitudes the aircraft would use and there's visible moisture (clouds/precip), assume a likely decline.

Where icing shows up — products to check
ProductWhat it tells you
AIRMET ZuluForecast moderate icing + freezing levels over a wide area. A broad advisory, not a guarantee — but a flag to check closer.
SIGMET (icing)Severe icing. Treat as a hard no-go for non-FIKI aircraft.
PIREPActual pilot reports of ice (e.g. "ICG 060" = icing at 6,000 ft). The most concrete signal.
Forecast Icing (FIP) chartGraphical icing probability/severity by altitude on aviationweather.gov.
Freezing levelListed in AIRMET Zulu and on the GFA. The lower it is, the smaller the ice-free operating window.

Rule of thumb for the screen: freezing level near the surface + clouds, or any icing SIGMET → treat as Low availability, commit ground. AIRMET Zulu with a high freezing level and otherwise clear skies → Uncertain, hold and let the program decide. When unsure, the flight program's pilot makes the icing call against their aircraft's certification — your job is to recognize the likely decline early.

FAA Flight Category — Quick Cross-Check

Aviationweather.gov and most apps color-code each station by flight category, set by whichever is worse — ceiling or visibility. It's a fast sanity check that lines up with the screen above: IFR/LIFR ≈ below HAA VFR minimums (commit ground), MVFR ≈ marginal (hold), VFR ≈ likely available (may release).

Caveat: flight category is a general aviation classification, not the HAA rule. A station can read MVFR yet still be below a specific program's company minimums, and the category says nothing about thunderstorms — so a VFR-category field with VCTS is still a no-go. Use category as a fast cross-check, not a replacement for the screen and the convective gate.

FAA Weather Minimums — Reference

HEMS ops fall under 14 CFR Part 135, Subpart L. These are the regulatory floor; programs often fly to higher company minimums in their OpSpecs, so a flight can be declined even when these are met. The Corbin area is largely Class G uncontrolled airspace, so § 135.609 governs most local launches.

On the 800 ft figure: an IFR approach to a hospital pad (typically a Point-in-Space / PinS approach) brings the aircraft to a published minimum descent altitude, after which the pilot needs the required flight visibility to proceed VFR to the pad. The exact decision altitude/visibility come from the published plate for the Baptist Health Corbin pad — confirm with your HEMS provider rather than relying on a memorized number. Per § 135.613, on a "Proceed VFR" segment of 1 NM or less, flight visibility must be ≥ 1 SM and the chart ceiling applies.

Tip — decode the raw code: on any aviationweather.gov METAR page, use the "Decoded" toggle (vs. "Raw") to flip the raw string like OVC007 1 1/2SM into plain English — ceiling, visibility, wind, and weather spelled out. Handy if you're not used to reading raw METARs under pressure. The live cards above already decode ceiling, visibility, and category for you.
Reading a METAR fast: ceiling = lowest BKN/OVC layer (OVC007 = 700 ft AGL); FEW/SCT are not ceilings. Visibility = number before SM. Convection: TS = thunderstorm at the field (within 5 SM), VCTS = vicinity (5–10 SM), LTG DSNT NE in remarks = distant lightning to the NE (10–30 NM), TS MOV NE = storm moving northeast. So OVC005 3/4SM +TSRA = 500 ft, 3/4 mile, heavy thunderstorm rain — a clear no-go on multiple counts.
